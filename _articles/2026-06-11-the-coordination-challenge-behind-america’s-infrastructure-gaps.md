Across the country, conversations about infrastructure tend to follow a familiar script. We need to build more. Harden what we have. Invest at a larger scale.

In many cases, those things are true. But they are not the whole story—and in some cases, they are not even the primary constraint.

Because in complex systems, performance rarely breaks down for lack of components alone. It breaks down when those components—policies, operators, markets, and physical assets—are not working in concert.

What often appears as an infrastructure shortfall is frequently the downstream result of coordination challenges that have gone unresolved.

This does not mean infrastructure investment is unnecessary. In many places, it is urgent. But the value of those investments depends heavily on whether they are planned, sequenced, and integrated around the needs of the system as a whole.

Nowhere is this more evident—or more consequential—than in the electric system.

Electricity is becoming the backbone of modern economic life in new ways. It is central to climate strategy, yes, but also to something more immediate and broadly felt: reliability, resilience, and affordability.

Communities are asking practical questions. Can the system hold up during extreme weather? Will costs remain manageable? Are there ways to reduce dependence on distant, centralized sources of energy?

These are not abstract concerns. They are rooted in a desire for stability, greater local control, and a measure of self-reliance—particularly in rural and geographically dispersed regions where outages and price volatility are felt most acutely.

What makes this moment distinct is that many of the tools needed to address these concerns already exist.

Distributed energy resources. Smarter demand management. More flexible system operations. Better visibility into real-time conditions across the grid.

The challenge is not waiting for a breakthrough technology.

The challenge is ensuring these capabilities are deployed and used in ways that reinforce the system, rather than merely adding to its complexity.

Today’s electric grid was not designed for this level of complexity. It evolved around large, centralized generation, predictable demand patterns, and relatively linear flows of power. Planning, regulation, and operations were built around that model—and in many places, still reflect it.

But the system is now being asked to do something fundamentally different.

Power is increasingly generated closer to where it is used. Demand is more dynamic. Extreme weather introduces new stresses. And decisions are being made across a wider set of actors, each operating with their own incentives, timelines, and constraints.

In that environment, infrastructure gaps often emerge—but they are not always the starting point. More often, they are the result of misalignment over time: investments that were not coordinated, signals that did not reflect system needs, or decisions made in isolation from operational realities.

Simply adding more infrastructure does not necessarily resolve these underlying issues. In some cases, it can even compound them—introducing additional complexity without improving how the system functions as a whole.

What matters more is how well the pieces fit together.

Where coordination is strong, systems tend to perform with greater flexibility and resilience. Small adjustments—made earlier and with better information—can prevent larger disruptions later. Local resources can be integrated in ways that reduce strain on the broader system. Costs can be managed more effectively by avoiding unnecessary or poorly timed investments.

Where coordination is weak, the opposite occurs. Systems become reactive. Stress accumulates in ways that are not always visible until they become difficult to manage. The result is often more expensive infrastructure needs later—needs that might have been reduced, avoided, or more efficiently targeted through earlier coordinated intervention.

This is where the opportunity lies.

The next phase of progress in the electric sector is not solely about building more. It is about improving how decisions are made, how investments are sequenced, and how the system adapts in real time.

That requires a different kind of focus.

Not just on individual technologies, but on how they are integrated.

Not just on long-term planning, but on how decisions are made and adjusted in real time.

Not just on investment levels, but on whether those investments are reinforcing or fragmenting the system.

These are fundamentally coordination challenges.

And they are solvable.

They require identifying where policy signals, planning processes, stakeholder expectations, and operational realities are beginning to diverge—and correcting course before those gaps become more expensive to address.

They also require better alignment across policy frameworks, operational practices, and the incentives that shape behavior—but not in a way that demands perfect agreement or centralized control. Rather, in a way that allows different parts of the system to move in the same general direction, even as conditions change.

For communities, particularly in rural areas, this opens up a more practical and immediate path forward.

Greater resilience does not have to mean waiting for large-scale infrastructure projects. Affordability does not have to depend solely on external market forces. Greater local control does not require complete independence from the grid, but a more balanced and flexible relationship with it.

These are not distant possibilities. They are available within the current technological landscape—if the system can operate with greater coherence.

The conversation around infrastructure is not wrong. But it is incomplete.

The more useful question is not just what we build next, but how well we are coordinating the system that determines what needs to be built in the first place.

That is where the next set of gains will come from.
